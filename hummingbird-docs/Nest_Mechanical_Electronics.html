Executive Overview

Hummingbird Technologies is developing the Hummingbird Nest — the world's first containerized autonomous drone swarm platform. The Nest deploys, manages, and recovers a fleet of 20–30 AI-powered Hummingbird drones from a self-contained modular container, enabling persistent aerial coverage for defense, law enforcement, emergency response, and municipal services.

The Hummingbird Nest Platform is a containerized, modular system capable of deploying, coordinating, and retrieving up to 30 configurable autonomous drones in coordinated swarm missions. The container houses vertical cassette-style drone storage, autonomous retrieval via six-axis robotic arm, and a game-like mission control interface powered by a ROS-based distributed control architecture. The Nest container can be deployed on a variety of platforms — including pickup truck beds, flatbed vehicles, maritime vessels, or stationary installations — and accepts external power or can be paired with a power module (solar, generator) for self-sufficient operation. For law enforcement and rapid-response applications, the preferred deployment configuration mounts the container on a plug-in hybrid electric vehicle (PHEV) with 7+ kW power export capability. The entire system is operable by a single operator.

The platform provides seven kilowatts of total system power for drone charging, computation, and mechanical operations. Each drone features a compact ducted coaxial quad-rotor propulsion system [20] within a standardized cassette form factor, delivering approximately 23–30 minutes of flight endurance per charge cycle. The system maintains 100% continuous swarm uptime through intelligent drone rotation—automatically cycling fresh drones out to replace those returning for recharge, ensuring uninterrupted mission coverage.

System coordination is managed by three specialized software modules: a Mission Manager tracking objectives and progress, a Swarm Manager handling drone rotation and health monitoring, and a Ground Manager orchestrating physical launch, retrieval, and charging operations. The coordinated airspace management model is inspired by Air Traffic Control principles.

Systems-Level Technical Highlights

  • Containerized modular platform: Self-contained unit deployable on trucks, flatbeds, maritime vessels, or stationary installations; multiple Nest containers are stackable and combinable for larger configurations
  • Single-operator system: One person operates the entire platform including mission planning, swarm management, and drone recovery — no piloting skill required
  • 30 drones per container, stored in vertical cassette slots per drone
  • Ducted coaxial quad-rotor propulsion: four ducts with pusher-puller counter-rotating props per duct (8 motors total per drone), fully enclosed within cassette form factor
  • ~23–30 minute flight endurance per drone (6S high-density LiPo, 230 Wh/kg); chemistry-agnostic battery bay designed for upgrade path to 300+ Wh/kg
  • 100% continuous swarm coverage through automated drone rotation—Swarm Manager proactively cycles fresh drones before returning units reach depletion
  • Flexible power: Accepts external power (preferred), PHEV power export, or paired power modules (solar, diesel/gas generator) for self-sufficient operation
  • Dormant-to-active readiness: System can remain in pre-positioned dormant state with automated battery maintenance cycling, then activate remotely on command for immediate operations
  • Day/night and all-weather operations: Operational in standard weather conditions (moderate wind, light rain, light snow, normal temperature ranges); swarm supports mixed sensor payloads including visual, low-light, IR, and thermal cameras for 24/7 capability
  • Three-module distributed control: Mission Manager, Swarm Manager, and Ground Manager as coordinated ROS nodes
  • Operator-on-the-loop and operator-in-the-loop: Autonomous swarm mission execution (operator-on-the-loop) with seamless transition to direct manual control of any individual drone (operator-in-the-loop) and back
  • Dual-computer architecture per drone: Pixhawk 6X flight safety + NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano 67 TOPS AI
  • Dual communication system: LTE cellular and mesh Wi-Fi networking with automatic fallback
  • Six-axis robotic arm with electromagnetic end effector for magnetic retrieval and automated charging
  • Soft-docking magnetic coupling system enabling rapid capture without precision alignment
  • RTK GPS centimeter-level navigation with GPS-denied capability via INS, visual-inertial odometry, and depth SLAM; cooperative swarm localization for enhanced positioning in degraded environments
  • Quad-channel communications for continuous link integrity; full DDIL (denied, degraded, intermittent, limited) resilience

2. Mechanical Architecture

2.1 Nest Containerized Platform

The Hummingbird Nest is fundamentally a self-contained, containerized autonomous drone system. The container houses all drone storage, launch/recovery mechanisms, charging infrastructure, robotic retrieval, computation, and communications in a single modular unit. The container is designed to be deployed on a variety of host platforms depending on operational requirements.

Container System Requirements:

  • Self-contained unit: All drone storage (30 cassette slots), robotic arm, launch mechanism, charging system, onboard computers (Mission Manager, Swarm Manager, Ground Manager), and power management housed within a single containerized enclosure
  • Drone storage: 30 drones in vertical cassette format distributed across front and side mounting areas (~10 per side)
  • Modular and stackable: Individual Nest containers are smaller than a standard 20-foot ISO container; multiple units can be combined side-by-side to fill a standard container footprint for logistics and transport
  • Standard power interface: Accepts external power input (preferred) with standard connectors; minimum 7 kW continuous power for drone charging, computation, and mechanical operations
  • Arm mounting: Six-axis robotic retrieval arm and integrated launch system with coordinated motor control
  • Environmental protection: Ruggedized enclosure for outdoor deployment in standard weather conditions

Deployment Configurations:

Flatbed / Utility VehicleContainer secured on flatbed for transport and field deploymentVehicle power export or paired generator

Power Flexibility:

  • External power (preferred): Shore power, ship power, or facility power via standard interfaces — no additional hardware required
  • PHEV power export: For vehicle-mounted configurations; 7+ kW continuous output enables silent electric operation and quick gas refills for extended deployments
  • Solar power module: Paired solar array for off-grid stationary deployments
  • Generator module: Diesel or gas generator for fully self-sufficient field operations

Dormant State & Remote Activation:

The Nest container is designed to operate in a pre-positioned dormant state for extended periods (days, weeks, or longer). During dormant mode, the system automatically manages battery cycling — periodically charging and discharging drone batteries to maintain optimal cell health and readiness. Upon receiving a remote activation command via the secure communication link, the system powers up to full operational status and is ready to launch drones without any on-site personnel. The entire system can be operated fully remotely from a ground control station.

Platform flexibility: The containerized architecture is inherently platform-agnostic. The same Nest container that mounts on a pickup truck for a police department can be placed on a naval vessel, pre-positioned on a building rooftop, or transported by cargo aircraft to a disaster zone. The only variable is the power source, which is addressed through the standard power interface and optional power modules.

2.2 Hummingbird Drone Design

Drones are engineered with a standardized cassette form factor that enables mechanical and electrical integration with the vehicle system. The cassette design is inspired by VHS/DVD cartridge storage for intuitive spatial organization and efficient retrieval. The entire drone—including propulsion, electronics, battery, payload, and communications—is fully integrated within the cassette envelope with no folding or deployment mechanisms required.

Form Factor Specifications:

  • Standardized form factor: 12' × 12' square face, approximately 4' thick per drone (scout); 18' × 18' × 5' (primary)
  • Fully integrated design: All propulsion, electronics, battery, payload, and communication systems contained within the cassette envelope—no folding arms or external appendages
  • Vertical slot mounting: VHS-style vertical cassette slots along front and left/right sides of the vehicle bed
  • Slot distribution: ~10 slots on front, 10 on left side, 10 on right side (total 30)
  • Standardized connectors: Mechanical connectors for robotic arm retrieval with soft-docking electromagnetic compatibility
  • Charging contacts: Integrated electrical contacts for automated charging distributed across 7 kW system power
  • Magnetic grasping points: Secure electromagnetic coupling with soft-docking tolerances
  • Onboard flight controller: Each drone runs its own autonomous flight controller for real-time decision-making
  • Modular center-mounted payload bay: Interchangeable sensors and tools selected based on mission type

Propulsion System

Each drone uses a ducted coaxial quad-rotor configuration optimized for the compact cassette form factor. Four ducted fan units are positioned at the corners of the airframe, with a central cross-shaped zone reserved for electronics, battery, and modular payload.

  • Configuration: 4 ducted fan units in corner-mounted arrangement within square envelope
  • Rotor type: Coaxial pusher-puller (counter-rotating) propeller pairs per duct—8 brushless motors total per drone
  • Propeller diameter (primary): 6 inches (152 mm) per duct
  • Duct outer diameter (primary): ~7 inches, pushed to corners of the 18' airframe
  • Center payload zone (primary): ~6'-wide cross-shaped area between the four corner ducts, housing flight controller, battery, comms, GPS, sensors, and modular payload bay
  • Motor class: 2207-class brushless motors (22 mm stator, 7 mm height), 6S compatible, 1400–1600 KV, ~30 g each
Ducted coaxial advantages: The ducted design provides ~15–25% thrust augmentation at hover vs. open props, improved safety with fully enclosed rotors (critical for operations near people), reduced acoustic signature, and propeller protection during cassette operations. The coaxial counter-rotating configuration eliminates torque reaction, doubles thrust density per duct footprint, and provides single-motor-failure redundancy per duct.

2.3 Robotic Retrieval System

A six-axis robotic arm with an electromagnetic end effector handles all drone launch and retrieval operations. The arm is dimensioned to reach all 30 cassette slots and extend beyond the vehicle perimeter for external drone capture and repositioning.

Technical Specifications:

  • Robotic arm: Six-axis (6-DOF) industrial-grade arm with coordinated motor control
  • Reach distance: 3.5–4 feet maximum, enabling access to all internal cassette slots and external drone capture
  • End effector: Electromagnet-based coupling system for secure drone grasping and soft-docking insertion
  • Electromagnetic coupling: Soft-docking design requiring no precision alignment; magnetic attraction provides centering force
  • Magnetic contact points: TBD based on load testing and grip force requirements; likely multiple points for redundancy
  • Load capacity: Sized for repeated launch/retrieval cycles over extended operations with thermal management
  • Control system: Coordinated with Ground Manager for sequenced launch and retrieval
  • Mechanical locking: Prevents unintended drone ejection during vehicle transit and maneuvers

2.4 Launch Mechanism

Drones are extracted from storage slots and positioned for autonomous takeoff. The launch process leverages the drone's onboard flight controller and propulsion systems, with the robotic arm serving as the positioning mechanism. The Ground Manager coordinates the launch sequence based on Swarm Manager requests.

Launch Sequence:

  1. Ground Manager receives launch request from Swarm Manager with specific drone ID and slot location
  2. Robotic arm retrieves selected drone from vertical cassette slot using electromagnetic coupling
  3. Arm positions drone in external launch area or hover-ready position above vehicle
  4. Electromagnetic coupling is de-energized, releasing magnetic attachment
  5. Drone autonomously activates flight controller and ducted coaxial propulsion based on flight plan from Swarm Manager
  6. Drone takes flight following mission instructions; arm retracts to neutral for next operation

2.5 Electromagnetic Capture & Proximity Landing

Precision landing is offloaded to the ground station. The Ground Manager uses multi-sensor fusion to guide drones from the vehicle's reference frame.

Magnetic Coupling

  • Drone: Permanent magnets in docking surface
  • Arm: Electromagnet end effector (energized for coupling)
  • Self-centering: ~2" radius in mating plane

Capture Dynamics

  • X-axis: Vehicle travel direction; YZ-plane: Arm holds fixed capture point
  • Tilt compensation: 6-axis arm tilts end effector to match drone attitude
  • Envelope: ~5-6 ft from vehicle; Ground Manager assumes authority via 900 MHz

Capture Sequence P1: Stationary P2: Moving

  1. Swarm Manager recalls drone; autonomous RTK GPS return
  2. Enters ~5-6 ft envelope; 900 MHz link established
  3. IR camera acquires IR LED signature
  4. Handoff: Ground Manager assumes guidance via 900 MHz to Pixhawk
  5. Ground-guided: Fuses RTK + camera; position/velocity commands
  6. Fine alignment: Camera primary at <2 ft; arm tilts to match attitude
  7. EM capture: Electromagnet energized within 2" tolerance; self-centers
  8. Motors off: Ground Manager commands shutdown via 900 MHz
  9. Arm retracts; inserts to slot; charging begins
Staging: P1 = stationary capture. P2 = moving vehicle capture with velocity matching + tilt compensation.

2.6 Charging System

Rapid charging occurs automatically during drone storage in cassette slots. The 7 kW power system is distributed intelligently across all 30 charging slots, computation, and mechanical operations.

Charging Architecture:

  • Power source: PHEV power export output (7 kW total system power)
  • Battery interface: 6S LiPo compatible charging contacts (22.2V nominal); chemistry-agnostic upgrade path
  • Intelligent distribution: Power management allocates 7 kW across charging slots, computation, and robotic arm based on real-time demand
  • Automatic charging: Begins immediately upon drone insertion into cassette slot with managed charge rate
  • Charging management: Monitors and optimizes charging across all simultaneous operations, preventing overload
  • Thermal management: Thermal monitoring and load balancing to prevent overheating during extended operations
  • Power budget redundancy: Gracefully handles simultaneous computation, charging, and arm operations within 7 kW budget

  • Primary user: Jetson
  • Self-healing: Auto-reroutes on link drop

  • Role: Dedicated short-range link for launch/capture operations

Design rationale: The 900 MHz "tractor beam" protocol connects directly to the Pixhawk, bypassing the Jetson for minimal latency. The Ground Manager guides the drone from its own reference frame where it knows the arm and slot positions.

  • Drone-side: RTK GPS receiver connected to Pixhawk (lowest latency); position relayed to Jetson via MAVLink

3.4 GPS-Denied & Degraded Navigation

While RTK GPS provides centimeter-level positioning under nominal conditions, the Hummingbird platform is designed to maintain full navigational capability when GPS is degraded, denied, or actively spoofed. This is critical for operations in urban canyons, near structures, in electromagnetically contested environments, or in areas subject to intentional GPS jamming.

Multi-Modal Navigation Stack

When GPS quality degrades, the system transitions to a multi-layered navigation approach that fuses multiple independent positioning sources. All navigation processing runs onboard the drone's main computer (Jetson Orin Nano), ensuring full autonomy with no dependency on ground station or cloud connectivity.

Navigation SourceMethodStrengthsLimitations
RTK GPSSatellite positioning with RTK correctionsCentimeter accuracy, absolute position, global referenceVulnerable to jamming, spoofing, urban canyon multipath
Inertial Navigation System (INS)Triple-redundant IMU integration (accelerometer + gyroscope)Immune to external interference, continuous output, no line-of-sight requirementsDrift accumulates over time without correction
Visual-Inertial Odometry (VIO)Camera-based motion estimation fused with IMU dataHigh accuracy in feature-rich environments, drift-boundedDegraded in low light, smoke, fog, or featureless terrain
Depth SLAMSimultaneous Localization and Mapping using depth sensors (stereo vision, LiDAR)3D environment mapping, robust in GPS-denied indoor/urban environmentsComputational cost, requires sufficient geometric features

Condition-Adaptive Sensor Fusion

The navigation system employs a condition-adaptive fusion architecture rather than a fixed priority hierarchy. Each navigation source continuously reports health metrics (signal quality, confidence, drift rate, feature density), and the fusion algorithm dynamically adjusts the weighting of each source based on real-time conditions:

  • Nominal GPS: RTK GPS is primary with high weighting; INS/VIO/SLAM provide cross-validation and smooth short-duration GPS dropouts
  • Degraded GPS: GPS weighting is reduced proportionally based on reported signal quality metrics; VIO and SLAM weighting increases to compensate
  • Denied GPS (jamming detected): GPS is fully excluded from the fusion; INS provides short-term continuity while VIO and depth SLAM assume primary positioning role
  • Spoofing detected: GPS module-level spoofing detection triggers full GPS rejection; system falls back to INS/VIO/SLAM stack with no GPS influence on position estimate
  • Visual degradation (smoke, low light, featureless): VIO weighting reduced; INS and depth SLAM (which functions independently of visible light) increase in priority
GPS integrity monitoring: The RTK GPS module itself provides signal integrity metrics and spoofing detection indicators. When anomalies are detected (position jumps, carrier-phase inconsistencies, unexpected signal strength patterns), the module flags the condition and the fusion algorithm responds proportionally — from reducing GPS weighting to fully discarding GPS output. This prevents spoofed GPS signals from corrupting the navigation solution.

Onboard Processing

All sensor fusion and navigation processing runs entirely on the drone's main computer (Jetson Orin Nano). The 67 TOPS AI compute capacity handles simultaneous VIO feature tracking, SLAM map construction, and INS integration at rates sufficient for real-time flight control. No ground station, cloud, or external processing is required for GPS-denied navigation — each drone maintains fully self-contained positioning capability.

Cooperative Localization (Swarm-Enhanced Positioning)

When operating as a swarm in GPS-denied environments, drones can improve their individual position estimates through cooperative localization. Each drone shares its position estimate and associated error covariance matrix over the WiFi mesh network. By combining multiple drones' independent estimates and their inter-drone measurements, the swarm achieves better collective positioning accuracy than any individual drone alone.

  • Shared error matrices: Each drone broadcasts its position estimate and error covariance; neighboring drones incorporate these into their own fusion, reducing overall position uncertainty
  • RF-based relative ranging: WiFi signal strength measurements between drone mesh modules provide relative distance estimates between swarm members, adding an additional constraint to the cooperative position solution
  • Potential additional RF ranging: Other onboard radio frequencies may also contribute relative distance measurements for further ranging redundancy
Graceful individual fallback: If environmental conditions become so severe that even the inter-drone WiFi mesh cannot be maintained, each drone reverts to fully independent navigation using its own INS/VIO/SLAM stack and continues its assigned mission autonomously. See Section 4.3.1: Swarm Operations in Denied Environments for details on swarm behavior under these conditions.

4.1 Distributed Manager Architecture

4.1.1 Mission Manager (Strategic) P1

Owns mission objectives; translates operator intent into directives for Swarm Manager.

  • Tracks progress, completion criteria, mission state
  • Issues high-level directives (e.g., "maintain coverage over zone A")
  • Handles abort, priority changes, objective updates
  • Triggers post-mission processing

Owns active fleet composition; maintains continuous coverage.

  • Translates directives into drone assignments, waypoints, formations
  • Proactive drone rotation: monitors battery, sensors, comms, performance
  • Flexible rotation: send first (critical) / recall first (non-urgent) / simultaneous
  • Manages inter-drone coordination and collective behavior
  • Multi-trigger recall: battery, sensor, comms, mechanical anomaly
  • Coordinates with Ground Manager for launch/retrieval

4.1.3 Ground Manager (Physical) P1

Manages all physical operations at the vehicle.

  • Robotic arm sequencing for launch and retrieval
  • Cassette inventory: slots, charge states, configs, maintenance
  • 7 kW power distribution with load balancing
  • Pre-flight checks and readiness reporting
  • Proximity capture authority: Guides returning drones via 900 MHz using sensor fusion
  • Arm attitude control: Tilts end effector to match drone pitch/roll

Mission Mgr → Swarm Mgr: "Continuous coverage zone A, thermal sensors"

Swarm Mgr → Ground Mgr: "Launch drone 14 (slot B3); recover drone 7 (slot A5)"

  • Independent safety decisions: hazards, comms loss, emergency landing
  • Capture mode: Direct commands from Ground Manager via 900 MHz
  • Dual watchdog: Monitors Jetson; safe mode on failure

4.3 Autonomous Swarm Coordination

Continuous Coverage Model

100% swarm uptime despite ~23–29 min individual endurance:

  • Proactive replacement: Fresh drone launched before active drone's battery is critical
  • Flexible rotation: Replacement before, after, or simultaneous with return
  • Multi-trigger recall: Battery, sensors, comms, mechanical
  • Seamless handoff: Replacement assumes returning drone's role
  • Overlapping transit: Simultaneous outbound/inbound operations

4.3.1 Swarm Operations in Denied Environments

The swarm coordination model extends to GPS-denied and communication-denied operating environments. The system employs a graceful degradation strategy that maintains maximum mission capability at each level of environmental challenge.

GPS-Denied Swarm Operations

When GPS is unavailable across the operating area, the swarm continues coordinated operations using the multi-modal navigation stack on each drone. Swarm-level capabilities in GPS-denied conditions:

  • Cooperative localization: Drones share position estimates and error covariance matrices over the mesh network, mutually refining each other's position accuracy. The combined position solution from multiple drones is more accurate than any individual estimate
  • RF-based relative positioning: WiFi signal strength and potentially other RF measurements between drones provide inter-drone distance estimates, enabling relative formation maintenance even without absolute positioning
  • Coordinated missions continue: Swarm Manager directives (formations, coverage zones, rotation schedules) remain in effect using the GPS-denied navigation solutions. Mission integrity is maintained

Communication-Denied Operations

When the communication link between drones and the Nest is lost, drone behavior is mission-dependent. Each mission profile includes predefined behavior for communication loss scenarios:

  • Autonomous mission completion: For missions such as reconnaissance, where full objectives can be achieved without real-time operator input, drones continue executing the pre-loaded mission plan, capture all data (video, sensor readings, telemetry) to onboard storage, and return to base upon mission completion or when battery thresholds are reached
  • Partial mission continuation: For missions where some objectives can be completed autonomously but others require operator acknowledgment or input, drones complete achievable objectives and defer others pending communication restoration
  • Return to home: For missions requiring continuous operator input or where no achievable autonomous objectives remain, drones return to the Nest after a configurable timeout period

Store-and-Forward Data Model

When communications are unavailable, all mission data (video recordings, sensor captures, telemetry logs) is stored onboard the drone's local storage. Upon return to the Nest or restoration of the communication link, data is automatically transferred for processing, viewing, and archival. When communications are available throughout a mission, all data streams in real time to the ground station and any connected remote viewers.

4.4 Proximity Landing Protocol

Authority handoff from Swarm Manager to Ground Manager during capture:

  1. Swarm Manager recalls drone via LTE/mesh
  2. Drone navigates toward vehicle (Swarm Manager guidance)
  3. Enters envelope; 900 MHz link established
  4. Authority transfer: Swarm Manager hands off; Ground Manager acknowledges and takes guidance
  5. Ground Manager commands Pixhawk directly via 900 MHz; Swarm Manager monitors only
  6. Capture completes; Ground Manager sends "complete" to Swarm Manager
  7. Fleet inventory updated; charging begins

4.5 Mission Planning Interface

The Nest's native interface is a TAK-class situational awareness and command platform — operators familiar with ATAK, WinTAK, iTAK, or WebTAK will find the interface immediately recognizable. The map-centric common operating picture, blue force tracking, collaborative overlays, real-time video streaming, and multi-platform access mirror the TAK design philosophy used by over 500,000 military and public safety operators. The interface additionally draws design inspiration from strategy games (Civilization, Age of Empires, Factorio) for intuitive swarm orchestration.

TAK-Class Core Capabilities

  • Map-centric common operating picture (COP) with online and offline mapping
  • Real-time blue force tracking of all drones, the Nest container, and operator positions
  • Collaborative mapping with mission zone overlays, coverage cone visualization, and geospatial markup
  • Live video streaming from any drone in the swarm; multi-viewer access
  • Multi-platform: tablet, laptop, desktop, phone (PWA); remote access via LTE/cloud
  • Mobile client mode: view-only access for responding units (e.g., fire apparatus en route)
  • Cursor on Target (CoT) protocol integration for full TAK ecosystem interoperability — drone positions, sensor data, and mission information published to TAK Servers and visible on all TAK clients (ATAK, WinTAK, iTAK, WebTAK, TAKX)

Hummingbird-Specific Capabilities (Beyond Standard TAK)

  • Click-to-command swarm orchestration with mission type context menu
  • Area selection tools: draw polygons, rectangles, or freeform boundaries for coverage zones
  • Automated coverage analysis: system identifies optimal drone positions for selected area
  • Sensor fusion overlay: multi-drone thermal, visual, and environmental data composited into unified geographic display
  • Real-time telemetry per drone: position, battery, signal, altitude, speed
  • Drone rotation lifecycle dashboard: active, returning, in capture sequence, charging, queued for relaunch
  • Multi-tier manual control: seamless transition between autonomous swarm (operator-on-the-loop) and direct stick control of any individual drone (operator-in-the-loop)
  • Live capture monitoring with camera feed of robotic arm operations
  • Swarm health management: aggregate fleet metrics (battery distribution, link quality, sensor health, rotation efficiency)
